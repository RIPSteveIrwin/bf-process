03 — The brief

The legal spine, and why Dale closes.

Three points carry the negotiation. All verified against secondary sources on 19/06/2026; Stephen/Counsel confirm on Westlaw before pleading.

Pillar I

Force the sale

A co-owner has an absolute right to division and sale of the whole farm — no equitable defence. Upper Crathes Fishings v Bailey's Exrs 1991 SLT 747.

Nuance: the court chooses the mode (division or sale); for an integrated farm, sale is the realistic outcome.

Pillar II

No forced buyout

A court cannot order Dale to buy Peter out (or vice-versa). The buyout happens by agreement, under the pressure of the sale. Collins v Sweeney [2013] ScotSC 20 (appeal refused 13/03/2014).

Pillar III

Make delay expensive

A formal pursuer's offer Dale fails to beat exposes him to a 50% uplift on Peter's post-offer expenses. SSI 2017/52. Not automatic (granted on cause shown); excludes outlays.

Why Dale closes above £500k

He is already at £500k. The marginal £200k–£300k buys him certainty of keeping the farm (vs open-market roulette), sole ownership of the turbine reversion and re-powering rights, vacant possession of the whole estate including Failte House, no forensic accounting of company-routed income, no challenge to the corporate sweep, ~£100k+ of avoided costs, and timing/tax control. That trade is cheaper than the fight.
